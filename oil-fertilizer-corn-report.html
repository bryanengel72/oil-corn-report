Executive Summary

The February 28, 2026 U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran and the subsequent closure of the Strait of Hormuz have triggered a polycrisis that connects energy markets, fertilizer supply chains, and global food security in ways that are only now becoming clear. Oil prices have surged past $105 per barrel, but the bigger long-term threat is the stranglehold on fertilizer flows arriving right as the Northern Hemisphere's spring planting season begins. Nearly a third of globally traded nitrogen fertilizer and roughly half of seaborne sulfur exports normally transit the Strait. Urea prices jumped 32% in a single week. If this disruption extends through April, the cascading consequence falls hardest on corn -- the crop underpinning the entire modern food, fuel, and livestock system. When corn falters, almost everything else does too.

Section 01

The Oil Disruption: Immediate Shock, Structural Fragility

The Strait of Hormuz has long been recognized as the world's most critical energy chokepoint. Nearly 20% of global seaborne oil trade transits this narrow waterway, carrying crude from Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, and Qatar to energy-hungry markets in Asia and Europe [1]. When the U.S. and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury on February 28, 2026, Iran responded by effectively closing the strait. Within days, tanker traffic had dropped by over 70%, with more than 150 vessels anchored outside the Persian Gulf unable to secure war-risk insurance [2].

Brent crude surged from roughly $71 per barrel on February 27 to $94 per barrel by March 9, with prices climbing toward $105 by mid-March as market participants priced in the very real possibility of a prolonged closure [3]. The IEA took the extraordinary step of releasing 400 million barrels from strategic reserves -- a move that underscores the gravity of the situation. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have some ability to reroute exports via pipeline to Red Sea ports, but all other Gulf producers are almost entirely dependent on the strait [2]. For Japan, which gets roughly 70% of its Middle Eastern crude via Hormuz, the crisis is existential at the supply level [2].

"Even with Iran's military degraded, it's easy for them to send a drone or two, drop a mine, or deliver a close-range missile."

U.S. President Donald Trump, March 2026 [2]

What makes this moment particularly precarious is that it lands on top of a structural oil-market dynamic that was already unstable. Throughout 2025, global oil markets swung between a glut scenario (supply outpacing demand by an estimated 2.3 million barrels per day for the year) and warnings from major producers that the underlying reserve replacement rate was dangerously low [4, 5]. Occidental CEO Vicki Hollub warned in early 2024 that the world had replaced less than 50% of the crude extracted over the prior decade, predicting a structural supply shortage by late 2025 [6]. That warning now looks prescient. Oil production capacity is concentrated in aging fields -- over 90% of upstream investment since 2019 has gone toward maintaining existing production, not expanding it [7].

Geopolitical Risk Factor

Even partial reopening of the strait does not resolve the crisis. War-risk insurance must be reinstated, and vessels require time to resume normal routes. Iran has signaled it may selectively allow non-Western shipping through, but drone attacks on Omani bypass ports at Duqm and Salalah have closed those alternative corridors as well [2]. The crisis is not simply military -- it is also logistical, legal, and financial.

Section 02

The Fertilizer Crisis: Oil's Hidden Cousin

The global fertilizer crisis emerging from the Hormuz closure is in many ways more alarming than the oil price spike -- and far less covered. The reason is structural. Natural gas is the essential feedstock for nitrogen fertilizers. Ammonia synthesis -- the backbone of urea and most nitrogen products -- requires hydrogen derived from natural gas, combined under pressure with atmospheric nitrogen. When gas supplies are disrupted or prices spike, fertilizer plants shut down. Fertilizer production in India, Bangladesh, and Pakistan has already been curtailed because those countries cannot obtain LNG from Qatar -- which exports its gas through the same strait that is now closed [8].

The scale of the Hormuz dependency is staggering and almost never discussed in mainstream energy reporting. Approximately one-third of all globally traded nitrogen fertilizer and nearly half of all globally traded sulfur normally transit the strait [8, 9]. Sulfur, largely an oil and gas byproduct, is an essential ingredient in manufacturing phosphate fertilizers -- meaning that the closure is disrupting both nitrogen and phosphate supply chains simultaneously. Gulf producers including Qatar Fertiliser Company, Saudi Arabia's SABIC, and the UAE's Fertiglobe are among the world's largest fertilizer exporters, and their cargoes are now physically stranded [10].

"Because fertilizer has less value than oil and gas, political and business leaders expend fewer resources to make sure it keeps flowing. A ship captain bold enough to brave drone strikes would prefer to carry oil."

Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, March 2026 [8]

The price signal is already extreme. Urea prices at the New Orleans import hub -- the U.S.'s key inland fertilizer benchmark -- jumped from $516 per metric ton on February 27 to $683 on March 5, a 32% increase in a single week [11]. Oxford Economics raised its Q2 2026 fertilizer price forecast by roughly 20%, with risks described as "skewed to the upside" [10]. One ton of urea now costs U.S. farmers the equivalent of 126 bushels of corn, up from 75 bushels just three months ago [11]. That ratio essentially means farmers must sell more crop just to afford the input that grows the next crop.

Why the 2026 Fertilizer Crisis Differs from 2022

  • Timing is worse. The strait closed right before the Northern Hemisphere spring planting window. Fertilizer delayed now misses the season entirely.
  • No emergency rerouting. Saudi Arabia's Red Sea pipeline is for oil, not ammonia. There is no alternative fertilizer export corridor.
  • No strategic reserves. G7 countries maintain oil stockpiles but have no strategic fertilizer reserves to release [8].
  • Second-order shutdowns. Producers in India, Pakistan, and Egypt that depend on Gulf gas are also curtailing output [8].
  • China restricting exports. China, the world's largest fertilizer producer, responded to the crisis by tightening export controls on urea and nitrogen-potassium blends -- exactly the opposite of what the world needs [9].

The historical parallel most analysts reach for is Sri Lanka in 2021-2022. When Sri Lanka banned synthetic fertilizers, domestic agriculture collapsed within a season, rice production fell by 20%, food prices doubled, and the government fell [8]. Sri Lanka's example is instructive not as a prediction of what will happen everywhere, but as a demonstration of the speed with which the fertilizer-to-food-price link can tighten when supply is abruptly removed. The 2022 Russia-Ukraine crisis produced a global fertilizer shock -- but that crisis unfolded over months and allowed for partial supply rerouting. The Hormuz closure is faster and hits a narrower geographic bottleneck with no bypass.

Section 03

Corn: The Linchpin Crop No One Thinks About Until It's Gone

Corn -- or maize -- is not primarily a food for most Americans. That is the crucial misconception. Of the roughly 15-17 billion bushels produced annually in the United States (a record was set in 2025 at 16.8 billion bushels on 98.7 million acres), roughly 40% goes to ethanol production and another 36-40% goes to livestock feed [12, 13]. Only a small fraction reaches humans directly, mostly as high-fructose corn syrup, corn starch, and corn-based processed food ingredients. Yet when corn supply tightens or corn prices spike, the consequences ripple through virtually every other food category -- because everything downstream depends on it.

The fertilizer connection is direct and critical. Corn is one of the most nitrogen-intensive crops grown at scale. Fertilizers account for roughly 36% of a corn farmer's operating costs [13]. When nitrogen prices surge 30% or more, as they have in March 2026, farmers face a stark choice: absorb the cost, reduce application rates (and accept lower yields), switch to less nitrogen-intensive crops, or not plant at all. Researchers already note that farmers may shift away from corn to crops like soybeans -- which fix their own nitrogen -- if input costs remain elevated through the planting window [11].

The Corn Dependency Chain -- What Breaks When Corn Falters

  • Livestock & meat. Roughly 36-40% of U.S. corn feeds cattle, hogs, and poultry. Feed costs represent 65-75% of poultry production expenses. A corn price spike directly translates to more expensive beef, pork, chicken, eggs, and dairy [14].
  • Ethanol & fuel. About 40% of U.S. corn goes to ethanol. Reduced corn supply tightens ethanol margins and can affect gasoline blend economics, compounding the oil-price hit already underway [12].
  • Processed food. High-fructose corn syrup, corn starch, corn oil, citric acid, dextrose, and dozens of other corn derivatives appear in an enormous share of packaged and processed foods. Any significant corn shortage raises input costs across the entire processed food industry [14].
  • Pharmaceuticals & industrial inputs. Corn-derived glucose and fermentation substrates are used in pharmaceutical manufacturing. Corn starch is used in paper, textiles, adhesives, and biodegradable plastics [13].
  • Export markets. The U.S. accounts for roughly 36% of global corn exports. Countries including Mexico, Japan, South Korea, Colombia, and several African nations depend on U.S. corn for food security [13].

The caloric math tells a stark story. An average Iowa cornfield has the theoretical potential to deliver more than 15 million calories per acre per year. But because so much corn is routed through ethanol and animal feed -- both of which are inefficient caloric converters -- the system delivers only about 3 million calories of food per acre. A single cow consumes a staggering amount of corn to produce a pound of beef, and only about one-third of the calories fed to livestock ultimately reach human consumption [15]. This inefficiency means that even a modest reduction in corn output creates outsized stress on the human food supply, because the margin is already thin.

"Without strategically prioritizing the delivery of critical farm inputs such as urea, ammonia, nitrogen, phosphate, and sulfur, the U.S. risks a shortfall in crops. This is a threat to our food security -- and by extension our national security."

American Farm Bureau Federation, letter to Trump Administration, March 2026 [11]

Right now, U.S. corn is actually in a surplus position -- 2025 was a record harvest year with roughly 2 billion bushels in ending stocks [16]. That buffer provides some insulation for the spring 2026 planting season. But it does not eliminate the risk. If fertilizer prices stay elevated through April, planting decisions shift. Reduced nitrogen application rates show up as yield declines six months later. And for the global farmers who do not enjoy the same cushion -- Brazil imports over 80% of its fertilizers and relies heavily on Gulf supply; India is facing shutdowns in its own fertilizer production -- the consequences of the current disruption will appear in harvest data by late 2026 and into 2027 [9].

Section 04

The Cascade: From Wellhead to Grocery Aisle

The chain of consequences from oil disruption to food prices is not linear -- it is multiplicative. Oil prices affect diesel costs for farm equipment and freight. High diesel means more expensive planting, harvesting, and distribution. It also means higher feedstock costs for nitrogen fertilizer. Higher fertilizer costs suppress corn planting. Lower corn supply tightens the livestock feed market. Higher feed costs raise meat, dairy, and egg prices. Simultaneously, higher diesel raises the cost of transporting every other food product. The result is a broad-based food inflation shock that arrives at the grocery store in waves -- fuel costs almost immediately, fertilizer impacts in the first and second harvests after disruption, and livestock and processed food impacts within six to twelve months of a sustained corn supply shock [17].

As of March 2026, all of these mechanisms are in motion simultaneously. Brent crude above $105 per barrel is already driving diesel above $5 per gallon in much of the U.S. [2]. Freight costs have climbed an estimated 20% since February, creating regional disruptions in the livestock supply chain as the cost of moving cattle to distant processing plants becomes economically prohibitive [18]. The USDA has noted a spike in "panic culling" -- ranchers liquidating herds early rather than paying escalating feed costs -- which creates a short-term meat price depression followed by a medium-term scarcity as herd sizes shrink [18]. The U.S. cattle herd was already at a 75-year low entering 2026, meaning there is little slack in the system to absorb another shock [18].

Beyond food prices, the geopolitical dimension of food insecurity is well-documented. The 2022 fertilizer and grain shock -- driven by Russia's invasion of Ukraine -- contributed to food price spikes that hit developing nations in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Middle East disproportionately hard [17]. Over 2.3 billion people worldwide already faced moderate or severe food insecurity as of 2023 -- a 45% increase from 2015 levels [17]. Countries like Yemen, where 17 million people were already approaching catastrophic food insecurity levels before this crisis, are now facing a double shock of supply chain disruption and elevated prices [10]. Lebanon, whose 2022-era food crisis never fully resolved, is facing a potential escalation affecting nearly a million people [10].

What the World Bank and FAO Are Watching

The FAO has warned explicitly that a prolonged Strait of Hormuz closure could place over 100 million people at risk of a humanitarian food catastrophe. The World Bank has previously modeled that high fertilizer prices in 2023 could lead to 1 million additional deaths from undernourishment. The 2026 disruption is happening faster, at a more critical planting moment, and with fewer strategic reserves available than in 2022 [10, 17].

Section 05

Outlook and Strategic Implications

The immediate question is whether the Strait of Hormuz reopens -- and how quickly. Iran has shown selective willingness to allow non-Western shipping through, and negotiations via Turkey and Iraq have produced some movement [2]. But the physical and insurance infrastructure required for normal shipping volumes to resume could take weeks even after a ceasefire or political agreement. Damage to bypass ports at Duqm and Salalah further limits alternatives. The spring planting window does not pause for geopolitics -- every day of delay past early April reduces the fertilizer available to farmers in the critical growing window.

The medium-term structural picture is also concerning. Oil investment has been declining in real terms for years. Saxo Bank and the EIA both project flat-to-declining U.S. shale output in 2026 if prices fall below $60 per barrel -- the irony being that a glut-driven price collapse could reduce the investment that prevents a shortage later this decade [4]. On fertilizer, the 2026 crisis is accelerating a conversation about strategic reserves and supply chain resilience that policymakers have deferred for decades. G7 governments maintain oil stockpiles but have no comparable fertilizer reserve mechanism. A single 30-day Hormuz closure, according to the U.N. Trade and Development Agency, can be enough to have a devastating impact on the food supply -- and the current disruption is already in its third week [9].

For the U.S. specifically, the picture is mixed but not dire in the short term. America produces roughly three-quarters of the fertilizer it consumes, and nitrogen producers like CF Industries have seen their stocks surge as domestic supply becomes more valuable [11]. U.S. corn ending stocks from the record 2025 harvest provide a meaningful buffer. But the interconnectedness of global food commodity markets means that international supply disruptions still ripple into domestic prices and export competitiveness. The American Farm Bureau Federation has already written to the White House urging that critical farm inputs be treated as a national security priority -- not just an agricultural market issue [11].

Key Signals to Watch

  • Strait reopening timeline. Every additional week of closure increases the probability that the 2026 corn and wheat planting season in the Northern Hemisphere is materially impacted.
  • China fertilizer export policy. A reversal of Beijing's export restrictions would meaningfully ease global supply. The current tightening is a compounding factor.
  • U.S. corn planting data. USDA planting intention reports in late March and April will signal how farmers are responding to elevated input costs.
  • South American harvest. Brazil and Argentina are in the late stages of their harvest cycle. Any shortfall there removes a key supply buffer for global corn markets.
  • War-risk insurance reinstatement. Commercial shipping cannot normalize even after military de-escalation until insurers reinstate coverage for the region.
  • India monsoon planting risk. With Indian urea production curtailed, the monsoon season planting (June-September) for rice and wheat is now at elevated risk.

The longer structural lesson here is one that food systems researchers have been raising for years: the global food supply chain is optimized for efficiency, not resilience. The nitrogen fertilizer system is simultaneously dependent on natural gas (an energy commodity), maritime shipping (a logistics system), and geopolitical stability (a foreign policy variable) -- all three of which are now under stress at the same time. Without strategic fertilizer reserves, without production diversification away from politically volatile regions, and without a rethink of the corn system's extraordinary concentration of risk across feed, fuel, and food, the next crisis will find the same vulnerabilities in the same places.

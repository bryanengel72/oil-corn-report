Executive Summary

The February 28, 2026 U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran and the subsequent closure of the Strait of Hormuz have triggered a polycrisis connecting energy markets, fertilizer supply chains, and global food security in ways only now becoming clear. Oil prices have surged past $105 per barrel, but the bigger threat is the stranglehold on fertilizer flows arriving right as the Northern Hemisphere spring planting season begins. Nearly a third of globally traded nitrogen fertilizer and roughly half of seaborne sulfur normally transit the Strait. Urea prices jumped 32% in a single week. If this disruption extends through April, the cascading consequence falls hardest on corn -- the crop underpinning the entire modern food, fuel, and livestock system.

Section 01

The Oil Disruption: Immediate Shock, Structural Fragility

The Strait of Hormuz has long been recognized as the world's most critical energy chokepoint. Nearly 20% of global seaborne oil trade transits this narrow waterway, carrying crude from Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, and Qatar to energy-hungry markets in Asia and Europe [1]. When the U.S. and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury on February 28, 2026, Iran responded by effectively closing the strait. Within days, tanker traffic had dropped by over 70%, with more than 150 vessels anchored outside the Persian Gulf unable to secure war-risk insurance [2].

Brent crude surged from roughly $71 per barrel on February 27 to $94 per barrel by March 9, with prices climbing toward $105 by mid-March [3]. The IEA took the extraordinary step of releasing 400 million barrels from strategic reserves. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have some ability to reroute exports via pipeline, but all other Gulf producers are almost entirely dependent on the strait [2].

Geopolitical Risk Factor

Even partial reopening of the strait does not resolve the crisis. War-risk insurance must be reinstated, and vessels require time to resume normal routes. Iran has signaled selective willingness to allow non-Western shipping through, but drone attacks on Omani bypass ports at Duqm and Salalah have closed those alternative corridors as well [2].

Section 02

The Fertilizer Crisis: Oil's Hidden Cousin

The global fertilizer crisis emerging from the Hormuz closure is in many ways more alarming than the oil price spike -- and far less covered. Natural gas is the essential feedstock for nitrogen fertilizers. Ammonia synthesis requires hydrogen derived from natural gas, combined under pressure with atmospheric nitrogen. When gas supplies are disrupted or prices spike, fertilizer plants shut down. Fertilizer production in India, Bangladesh, and Pakistan has already been curtailed because those countries cannot obtain LNG from Qatar [8].

Approximately one-third of all globally traded nitrogen fertilizer and nearly half of all globally traded sulfur normally transit the strait [8, 9]. Gulf producers including Qatar Fertiliser Company, Saudi Arabia's SABIC, and the UAE's Fertiglobe are among the world's largest fertilizer exporters, and their cargoes are now physically stranded [10].

"Because fertilizer has less value than oil and gas, political and business leaders expend fewer resources to make sure it keeps flowing. A ship captain bold enough to brave drone strikes would prefer to carry oil."

Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, March 2026 [8]

Urea prices at the New Orleans import hub jumped from $516 per metric ton on February 27 to $683 on March 5, a 32% increase in a single week [11]. One ton of urea now costs U.S. farmers the equivalent of 126 bushels of corn, up from 75 bushels three months ago [11].

Why the 2026 Crisis Differs from 2022

  • Timing is worse. The strait closed right before the Northern Hemisphere spring planting window.
  • No emergency rerouting. Saudi Arabia's Red Sea pipeline is for oil, not ammonia.
  • No strategic reserves. G7 countries maintain oil stockpiles but have no strategic fertilizer reserves [8].
  • Second-order shutdowns. Producers in India, Pakistan, and Egypt that depend on Gulf gas are also curtailing output [8].
  • China restricting exports. China tightened fertilizer export controls in response to the crisis [9].

Section 03

Corn: The Linchpin Crop No One Thinks About Until It's Gone

Corn is not primarily a food for most Americans. Of the roughly 15-17 billion bushels produced annually in the U.S. (a record 16.8 billion in 2025), roughly 40% goes to ethanol and another 36-40% goes to livestock feed [12, 13]. Fertilizers account for roughly 36% of a corn farmer's operating costs [13]. When nitrogen prices surge 30% or more, as in March 2026, farmers face a stark choice: absorb the cost, reduce application rates, switch crops, or not plant at all [11].

The Corn Dependency Chain -- What Breaks When Corn Falters

  • Livestock & meat. 36-40% of U.S. corn feeds cattle, hogs, and poultry. Feed costs = 65-75% of poultry production expenses [14].
  • Ethanol & fuel. ~40% of U.S. corn goes to ethanol. Reduced corn supply tightens ethanol margins [12].
  • Processed food. HFCS, corn starch, corn oil, citric acid, dextrose appear in an enormous share of packaged foods [14].
  • Pharmaceuticals & industrial. Corn-derived glucose is used in pharmaceutical manufacturing. Corn starch in paper, textiles, adhesives [13].
  • Export markets. The U.S. accounts for ~36% of global corn exports. Mexico, Japan, South Korea, Colombia depend on it [13].

An average Iowa cornfield has the potential to deliver more than 15 million calories per acre per year. But because so much corn flows through ethanol and animal feed, the system delivers only about 3 million food calories per acre -- lower than farms in Bangladesh, Egypt, and Vietnam [15]. This means even a modest reduction in corn output creates outsized stress on the human food supply.

"Without strategically prioritizing delivery of critical farm inputs such as urea, ammonia, nitrogen, phosphate, and sulfur, the U.S. risks a shortfall in crops. This is a threat to our food security -- and national security."

American Farm Bureau Federation, letter to Trump Administration, March 2026 [11]
Section 04

The Cascade: From Wellhead to Grocery Aisle

The chain of consequences from oil disruption to food prices is multiplicative. Oil prices affect diesel costs for farm equipment and freight. High diesel means more expensive planting and distribution. It also raises feedstock costs for nitrogen fertilizer. Higher fertilizer costs suppress corn planting. Lower corn supply tightens livestock feed markets. Higher feed costs raise meat, dairy, and egg prices. The result is a broad-based food inflation shock that arrives in waves [17].

As of March 2026, all these mechanisms are in motion simultaneously. Brent above $105 per barrel drives diesel above $5 per gallon [2]. Freight costs have climbed an estimated 20% since February [18]. The U.S. cattle herd was already at a 75-year low entering 2026 [18].

What the World Bank and FAO Are Watching

The FAO warned a prolonged Hormuz closure could place over 100 million people at risk of humanitarian food catastrophe. The World Bank previously modeled that high fertilizer prices in 2023 could lead to 1 million additional deaths from undernourishment [10, 17].

Section 05

Outlook and Strategic Implications

The immediate question is whether the Strait of Hormuz reopens -- and how quickly. Iran has shown selective willingness to allow non-Western shipping through. But the physical and insurance infrastructure required for normal shipping volumes could take weeks to restore even after a political agreement. The spring planting window does not pause for geopolitics [2].

For the U.S., the picture is mixed but not dire in the short term. America produces roughly three-quarters of the fertilizer it consumes, and U.S. corn ending stocks from the record 2025 harvest provide a meaningful buffer. But for Brazil, which imports over 80% of its fertilizers, and for India facing production curtailments, the consequences will appear in harvest data by late 2026 and into 2027 [9].

Key Signals to Watch

  • Strait reopening timeline. Every additional week of closure increases probability of 2026 planting season damage.
  • China fertilizer export policy. A reversal of Beijing's export restrictions would meaningfully ease global supply.
  • U.S. corn planting data. USDA planting intention reports in late March and April signal farmer response to high input costs.
  • South American harvest. Any shortfall in Brazil or Argentina removes a key global corn supply buffer.
  • War-risk insurance reinstatement. Shipping cannot normalize until insurers reinstate coverage for the region.
  • India monsoon planting risk. With Indian urea production curtailed, monsoon season planting (June-September) is at elevated risk.

The longer structural lesson is one that food systems researchers have been raising for years: the global food supply chain is optimized for efficiency, not resilience. The nitrogen fertilizer system is simultaneously dependent on natural gas, maritime shipping, and geopolitical stability -- all three of which are now under stress at the same time. Without strategic fertilizer reserves, without production diversification away from politically volatile regions, and without a rethink of the corn system's extraordinary concentration of risk, the next crisis will find the same vulnerabilities in the same places.
